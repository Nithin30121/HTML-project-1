Diwali — Festival of Lights

What is Diwali?

Diwali, the Festival of Lights, celebrates the victory of light over darkness and good over evil. Homes glow with diyas (lamps), streets sparkle with fireworks, and families exchange sweets and blessings.

The festival ties to several stories across India — including Lord Rama’s return to Ayodhya — and marks renewal, prosperity, and togetherness.

Rituals & Customs

Preparations include cleaning and decorating homes, lighting rows of oil lamps, making rangoli patterns, and conducting Lakshmi puja for abundance. Exchanging sweets and gifts strengthens family and community bonds.

Regional variations exist, but the central themes of light, hope, and new beginnings are universal.

Cultural Significance & Modern Celebrations

Diwali blends spiritual symbolism with lively public celebration: markets bustle, festivals light up cities, and cultural programmes bring communities together. It’s both a sacred time and a joyous social festival.

In modern India and across the global diaspora, Diwali also fosters cultural exchange and civic events.

Quick Facts

  • Usually occurs between October and November (Lunar calendar).
  • Associated with Lord Rama, Goddess Lakshmi, and Lord Ganesha in many regions.
  • Celebrated across communities with regional variations.
